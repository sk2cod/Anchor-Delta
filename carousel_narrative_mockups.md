# Carousel Narrative Mockups — Flowing Story Format

**Purpose:** Address viewer feedback that current carousels read as disconnected
facts rather than a story. No code, prompts, or templates were touched to
produce this — pure content mockups for review before anything downstream
changes.

**Source cards:** the most recent card per domain in Supabase as of this
review (world: `e2f8f875-c729-48b1-99f3-41baa02e4e6b`, finance:
`db16e2fb-84a1-4e9d-b541-7f34d612b387`, ai_tech: `f926c5d1-d3ad-430c-ae34-0e9ad62aeb4f`).

**Revision 2 — applied review feedback:** every non-obvious entity now gets
a one-phrase identifier on first mention and a short anchor on every repeat
mention (Rheinmetall, TKMS, Zelenskyy, Lockheed Martin, Anduril, General
Atomics, and the Klement repeat in Finance slide 7). The AI & Tech
carousel's closing slide was rewritten — the Australia/India angle was a
consequence of the story, not the story itself, so it's compressed into a
single universal closing line instead of holding its own slide. (Note: this
revision was written by hand for review; the entity-introduction rule itself
has also been added to the production writer prompt as `writer_v1_9.md`,
Decision #66, so future auto-generated carousels get this for free.)

**Principles applied to all three:**
- One story arc — each slide answers the question the previous slide raised, and raises the next one
- Headline + body are one continuous thought, not two separate facts
- Human register — stakes and consequences, not briefing prose
- No dates in body text (bare years for factual anchoring are fine — "founded in 2022" is not a dateline; "On July 7..." is)
- Numbers land inline, in the sentence that needs them — no separate numbers slide
- Quotes woven inline where they carry weight — no dedicated quote slide
- Non-obvious entities get a one-phrase identifier on first mention, and a short anchor (not a bare name) on every mention after that
- Regional angles only earn their own slide if the region IS the story — otherwise compress to one line or cut; the audience is global
- Length driven by the story, not a slot count — no proof slide, no forced minimum beyond making the arc actually land

## World — "Ukraine's War on Russia's Oil Economy"

*Arc: a striking distance fact → why the distance matters → why this exact target → the retaliation that doesn't actually threaten the strategy → why this week specifically → the humans behind and around it → the bigger frame.*

**1.**
**Headline:** A Ukrainian drone just flew farther than London to Cairo — into Russia.
**Body:** It landed in Siberia, a part of Russia that has never felt this war before. Until this week, that distance made it untouchable.

**2.**
**Headline:** That "untouchable" line just moved.
**Body:** Drone warfare comes down to one number — how far it can fly before it runs out of power. Ukraine's long-range drones used to stop well short of the Ural Mountains. A Kyiv defence company called Fire Point rebuilt its drones to close that gap. Every refinery east of the Urals is now in range.

**3.**
**Headline:** The target wasn't random. It was the biggest one available.
**Body:** Russia runs 11 major refineries that turn crude oil into usable fuel. Omsk is the largest of them, processing more than 21 million tonnes of crude a year. Hit Omsk, and you don't just start a fire — you choke the single biggest artery feeding Russia's war machine and its export income at the same time.

**4.**
**Headline:** Russia hit back within hours. It didn't matter.
**Body:** Missiles and drones tore into Kyiv the same night, killing at least 19 people. But Russia is already striking Ukrainian cities as hard as it's willing to go — there's nowhere left for it to escalate. Ukraine just proved it still has room to climb.

**5.**
**Headline:** The timing wasn't an accident either.
**Body:** Ukraine hit Omsk the day before leaders from 32 NATO countries sat down in Ankara to decide how much more support to give. Ukraine's President Zelenskyy didn't walk into that room asking for help. He walked in having just reached Siberia.

**6.**
**Headline:** "A significant blow to Russia's oil economy."
**Body:** That's Zelenskyy's own read on it — crediting Fire Point, the small Kyiv startup most people have never heard of, for putting Siberia within reach. In Washington, Trump told reporters a resolution is "getting closer than people realize." Two very different kinds of pressure, landing in the same week.

**7.**
**Headline:** This isn't a war for territory anymore. It's a war for Russia's cash.
**Body:** Every barrel Russia can't refine is a barrel it can't sell and can't burn in its own tanks. Ukraine figured that out — and it's working through Russia's refining map one strike at a time.

## Finance — "Canada's $100B Submarine Deal: Why NATO Won and South Korea Lost"

*Arc: a brutal stock-crash hook → why Canada actually made this choice → why it's not a one-off → the newest layer (Wall Street pricing the same shift) → the winners → the losers → the line that ties it together.*

**1.**
**Headline:** A South Korean shipbuilder lost a quarter of its value in one day.
**Body:** Hanwha Ocean was the front-runner to build Canada's next submarine fleet — a contract worth up to $100 billion. Then Canada said no. The stock cratered 23% overnight.

**2.**
**Headline:** Canada didn't pick the better submarine. It picked the safer ally.
**Body:** TKMS — a German shipbuilder — won the deal instead, and Canada's own government framed it as more than a hardware decision: NATO ties, Arctic capability, and not wanting your defence supply chain sitting outside the alliance when things get tense.

**3.**
**Headline:** This isn't a one-off. It's the new rule.
**Body:** Under pressure from Washington to spend more on their own defence, NATO countries are quietly pulling their biggest contracts back inside the alliance — even when a non-NATO supplier builds the better product. Loyalty is beating quality.

**4.**
**Headline:** Wall Street just started drawing the same line.
**Body:** Investors are no longer treating a rising defence budget as a blanket buy signal. They're asking a sharper question: is this company building software and drones, or still selling tanks?

**5.**
**Headline:** "These companies need to be traded like tech companies."
**Body:** That's strategist Joachim Klement's read on firms like Britain's BAE Systems and Italy's Leonardo — both building AI-enabled and drone warfare systems. Their valuations are starting to look less like defence stocks and more like software stocks.

**6.**
**Headline:** Rheinmetall builds tanks. That's now a problem.
**Body:** Rheinmetall, Germany's biggest defence manufacturer, is anchored to conventional weapons — and it's facing investor pressure despite record defence budgets. A bigger budget doesn't rescue a product built for a war that isn't being fought anymore.

**7.**
**Headline:** "Generals are fighting the previous war, not the next one."
**Body:** The strategist's own line cuts at the real issue — Europe's defence-industrial base is still dominated by companies built for conventional warfare. The money is moving toward whoever is actually building for the next one.

**8.**
**Headline:** Same forces, two different casualties.
**Body:** A South Korean shipbuilder lost a $100 billion contract because of where it's headquartered. A German tank maker is losing investor confidence because of what it builds. Both are learning the same lesson: alignment and relevance now matter more than size.

## AI & Tech — "The Undersea Arms Race: Lockheed Martin Swallows Ultra Maritime"

*Arc: an absurd-sounding price tag → why the underlying problem is so hard → why Lockheed actually wants it → the overlooked money story → the bigger technology shift → the universal consequence.*

**1.**
**Headline:** Lockheed Martin just paid $3.45 billion for something the size of a wine bottle.
**Body:** The world's largest defence contractor is betting big on a sonobuoy — a small, disposable sensor dropped into the ocean to listen for submarines. It just became one of the most valuable pieces of technology in modern warfare.

**2.**
**Headline:** Submarines are nearly invisible. That's the whole problem.
**Body:** They run silent, run deep, and can sit off a rival coast for weeks without being detected. The only way to find one is to flood the ocean with sensors — which is exactly what a sonobuoy is built to do.

**3.**
**Headline:** This isn't really a product purchase. It's a chokepoint purchase.
**Body:** Ultra Maritime is one of the world's leading sonobuoy makers. If every major Western navy needs them, and Lockheed now controls the supply, Lockheed controls a critical node in allied ocean defence — not just a catalogue item.

**4.**
**Headline:** Here's the part almost nobody's talking about.
**Body:** Private equity firm Advent bought Ultra Maritime in 2022 and spent $170 million rebuilding it into a fully independent supplier, then lined up flashy partnerships with Anduril, a fast-rising defence tech startup, and General Atomics, the drone maker, to make it look like the future of warfare. It worked — they sold it for a premium.

**5.**
**Headline:** Buy, build, package, sell — inside the defence industry.
**Body:** Advent ran the oldest private equity playbook there is. The only difference this time: the buyer was guaranteed by government demand, and the price tag had nine zeros.

**6.**
**Headline:** The bigger shift is drones taking over the ocean too.
**Body:** Old-school submarine hunting needed expensive crewed aircraft dropping sensors by hand. The new model pairs unmanned drones with AI-linked sonobuoy networks for constant, automated ocean surveillance at a fraction of the cost. Lockheed now owns both halves of that equation.

**7.**
**Headline:** One company just became harder to avoid.
**Body:** Every Western and allied navy hunting submarines now runs through more of the same supplier. Fewer independent vendors, less competitive pricing, and a single company sitting at the centre of the world's undersea defence — that's the real trade Lockheed just made.

## Notes for review

- None of these use a rigid hook/setup/pivot/proof/quote/cta slot structure — each carousel's length and shape came from what the story actually needed (7, 8, and 7 slides respectively).
- Numbers that used to warrant their own "fact sheet" slide (2,500km, 21 million tonnes, $100B, 23%, $3.45B, $170M) are all sitting inside the sentence that gives them meaning instead.
- Every quote used (Zelenskyy, Trump, Klement x2) is doing narrative work at the point it appears, not parked on an isolated attribution slide.
- The AI & Tech carousel's Australia/India detail — genuinely interesting, but a consequence for specific regions rather than the core story — is no longer its own slide. The underlying insight (fewer independent suppliers, more concentrated defence procurement) now closes the carousel at a level that applies to any reader, anywhere.
- Nothing here has been rendered, no images were generated, and no production file was touched by producing this document — this is content only, for review before any code or template changes are considered. The one exception, done separately and explicitly requested: the entity-introduction/anchoring rule itself was added to the real production writer prompt (`carousel/prompts/writer_v1_9.md`, Decision #66) since it's a standing rule, not something specific to this mockup exercise.
